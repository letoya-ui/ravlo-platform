Accessibility



Accessibility ensures that Ravlo is usable, readable, and navigable for all users — regardless of ability, device, or environment. It is a core requirement of the design system and a reflection of Ravlo’s commitment to clarity, structure, and emotional safety.



Ravlo’s accessibility standards are:

\- inclusive

\- predictable

\- readable

\- structured

\- calm

\- intentional



1\. Purpose

Accessibility guidelines ensure that every component, pattern, and surface in Ravlo meets or exceeds WCAG standards. They create a consistent, investor‑grade experience that is usable by everyone.



Accessibility defines:

\- contrast requirements

\- keyboard navigation

\- focus states

\- motion and animation rules

\- screen reader behavior

\- semantic structure



2\. Contrast

Ravlo uses a dark‑mode‑first palette with strict contrast rules.



Contrast requirements:

\- text must meet WCAG AA minimum contrast

\- primary text must be highly readable on all surfaces

\- semantic colors must remain accessible in all states

\- avoid low‑contrast combinations, especially on elevated surfaces



3\. Typography \& Readability

Typography must support clear, calm reading.



Rules:

\- maintain readable line lengths

\- use generous line height

\- avoid dense blocks of text

\- ensure hierarchy is clear for screen readers

\- avoid using color alone to convey meaning



4\. Keyboard Navigation

All interactive elements must be fully operable via keyboard.



Requirements:

\- tab order must follow logical reading flow

\- all controls must be reachable and operable

\- no keyboard traps

\- focus must be visible and predictable



5\. Focus States

Focus states must be clear, consistent, and accessible.



Rules:

\- use a visible focus ring on all interactive elements

\- maintain consistent focus styling across components

\- ensure focus contrast meets accessibility standards

\- avoid overly subtle or low‑contrast focus indicators



6\. Motion \& Animation

Motion must support clarity, not distract or overwhelm.



Guidelines:

\- avoid rapid or unexpected motion

\- provide reduced‑motion alternatives

\- use subtle, cinematic transitions

\- avoid flashing or high‑frequency animations



7\. Screen Readers \& Semantics

Ravlo must be fully navigable using assistive technologies.



Requirements:

\- use semantic HTML structure

\- ensure all interactive elements have accessible names

\- provide ARIA labels where needed

\- ensure dynamic content is announced appropriately

\- avoid redundant or noisy announcements



8\. Component Accessibility

Every component must follow accessibility rules.



Components must:

\- support keyboard and screen reader usage

\- maintain accessible contrast in all states

\- provide clear error and success messaging

\- avoid relying solely on color for meaning



9\. Do’s \& Don’ts



Do:

\- use clear labels and instructions

\- maintain predictable navigation

\- ensure all states are visually distinct

\- test with keyboard and screen readers

\- prioritize readability and clarity



Don’t:

\- use color alone to convey meaning

\- hide focus indicators

\- rely on hover‑only interactions

\- create dense or overwhelming layouts

\- use motion that distracts or disorients



Accessibility is a core part of Ravlo’s architectural identity. It ensures every user experiences clarity, structure, and confidence — regardless of ability or environment.
